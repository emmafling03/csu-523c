Step 2.1 Function to take an sf object and create a data frame

sum_tess <- function(arg1, arg2) {
  
  area_km2 <- arg1 %>%
    st_area() %>%
    set_units(km^2) %>%
    drop_units()
  
  data.frame(
    surface = arg2,
    n_features = nrow(arg1),
    mean_area_km2 = mean(area_km2),
    sd_area_km2 = sd(area_km2),
    total_area_km2 = sum(area_km2)
  )
}

The tessellations show clear differences in feature count and area, which directly affect point-in-polygon results. Counties and Voronoi polygons have similar mean areas, but counties show higher variability due to irregular boundaries, making them more susceptible to MAUP. Voronoi reduces some bias by using centroid-based proximity but still reflects uneven spatial distribution of counties.

Delaunay has the highest number of features andthe smallest mean area, allowing for finer spatial resolution and better detection of local patterns, but at increased computational cost. In contrast, square and hex grids have uniform areas, which improves consistency in analysis and reduces MAUP effects related to area variability. However, their coarser resolution (especially hex) may smooth over local detail.

Delaunay is most computationally expensive due to the high feature count, while hex grids are most efficient. Overall, finer tessellations capture more spatial detail but increase computational demand, while regular grids improve efficiency at the cost of resolution.

Step 3.2 Point in Polygon Function

pip_count <- function(arg1, arg2, arg3) {
  
  id_sym <- rlang::sym(arg3)
  
  counts <- st_join(arg2, arg1, left = TRUE) %>%
    st_drop_geometry() %>%
    count(!!id_sym, name = "n")
  
  out <- arg2 %>%
    left_join(counts, by = arg3) %>%
    mutate(n = replace_na(n, 0))
  
  return(out)
}

c. Which single tessellation would you recommend to a dam operations manager seeking to understand geographic clustering? Justify your choice by addressing: - Does the tessellation align with natural or management decision-making units (e.g., river basins, states, hydrologic regions)? - Does it reveal meaningful patterns without over-fragmenting the landscape or creating artificial boundaries? - How does MAUP influence your recommendation—are other tessellations equally valid, just for different questions? - How did simplification affect your choice? Would a coarser or finer simplification (different keep percentage) change your recommendation? - What does “best supports management decisions” mean concretely in your context—does it matter more that hot spots are obvious, or that boundaries align with jurisdictions?

As seen in the image above, several regions show clear differences based on tessellation choice. In the Southeast, Delaunay and Voronoi have localized hotspots, while the square and hex grids have broader areas of moderate density. This occurs because Delaunay has 6,198 tiles, the smallest mean area of 1290 km squared, and captures fine-scale variation. Meanwhile, grids have fewer tiles, uniform area, and aggregate counts over larger, consistent units. In the Midwest, Voronoi reveals clustered patterns with centroid spacing, while counties show more irregular patterns due to high area variability (sd 3443 km squared). In contrast, grid-based tessellations reduce this variability (sd = 0), producing more uniform and less detailed results. In the Western U.S., counties and Voronoi have clustering along river systems and developed corridors, while hex and square grids generalize these into larger regions, masking finer structure. This is due to geometry and tile size as Voronoi responds to centroid spacing, while grids have fixed spatial units. As shown in Q2, grids have larger mean areas (square at 2761 km squared, hex at 3781 km squared), which smooths local clustering. These differences illustrate MAUP, where changing the size and shape of spatial units alters the observed distribution of dams.

For a dam operations manager, I would recommend the hexagonal grid tessellation. It provides a good balance between detail and clarity. Counties have very uneven sizes, which can make patterns harder to understand, while square grids can introduce artificial, blocky patterns due to their alignment. On the other hand Delaunay creates many small tiles, which adds a lot of detail but can make the map harder to read. The hex grid avoids these issues by using consistent, evenly sized areas which makes it easier to see and compare regional clustering patterns. While it does not perfectly match natural boundaries like river basins, it still provides a clear and consistent way to visualize where dams are concentrated.

MAUP influences this decision, since different tessellations can show different patterns from the same data. No single method is “correct” as each highlights different aspects of the distribution. Delaunay is useful for identifying very fine-scale clustering, while counties may be more useful when decisions need to align with administrative boundaries. The simplification step (keep ≈ 0.02) only affects the outer boundary used for clipping and improves computational efficiency, so it does not change the main patterns seen in the tessellations. Overall, the hex grid is the best choice because it clearly shows meaningful hotspots without adding unnecessary complexity, making it easier for decision-makers to interpret and use.

Step 4.3 Geographic Distribution of Dams

The geographic distribution of dams generally makes sense and reflects known physical and human factors. Hydroelectric dams are concentrated in mountainous and high-flow areas such as the Pacific Northwest. Flood control dams are clustered in the central U.S., especially around the Mississippi River basin, where flood risk is higher. Irrigation dams are most common in the western United States, particularly in arid regions like California where water storage is critical for agriculture. Recreation dams are more widely distributed but show strong clustering near populated areas, where demand for recreation is high. These patterns align closely with major river systems, climate, and topography. Areas with dense river networks and higher precipitation have more flood control dams, while arid regions rely heavily on irrigation dam infrastructure. Mountainous regions support hydroelectric dams due to steeper gradients and higher energy potential. This confirms that dam placement is strongly tied to both natural hydrology and human water needs.

The hexagonal tessellation helps reveal these patterns clearly by using consistent spatial units, but it also smooths some local variation. Smaller, more detailed tessellations (like Delaunay) might show finer clustering along specific rivers, while larger or more uniform grids may generalize patterns across regions. This means that tessellation choice can influence how “clustered” dams appear, even though the underlying distribution is the same.

I would expect irrigation and hydroelectric dams to show different LISA hotspot patterns compared to other dams. Irrigation dams will likely cluster in the arid West, while hydroelectric dams will cluster in mountainous regions, leading to more specialized and region-specific hotspots.

Step 5.4 Global Interpretation of Spatial Patterns

Spatial Clustering Patterns:

The spatial lag correlations indicate that several dam attributes exhibit strong spatial clustering across neighboring hexagonal tiles. Dam count shows the strongest spatial dependence with a correlation of 0.837. This indicates that areas with many dams are typically surrounded by other high-density dam regions. make_lisa_objects <- function(sf_obj, value_col) {
  
  W <- st_touches(sf_obj, sparse = FALSE)
  diag(W) <- FALSE
  W_num <- W * 1
  
  rs <- rowSums(W_num)
  W_std <- W_num
  
  if (any(rs > 0)) {
    W_std[rs > 0, ] <- sweep(W_num[rs > 0, , drop = FALSE], 1, rs[rs > 0], "/")
  }
  if (any(rs == 0)) {
    W_std[rs == 0, ] <- 0
  }
  
  x <- sf_obj[[value_col]]
  lag_x <- as.numeric(W_std %*% x)
  
  z <- as.numeric(scale(x, center = TRUE, scale = TRUE))
  lag_z <- as.numeric(scale(lag_x, center = TRUE, scale = TRUE))
  
  list(
    W = W_num,
    W_std = W_std,
    row_sums = rowSums(W_std),
    isolated = sum(rowSums(W_std) == 0),
    x = x,
    lag_x = lag_x,
    z = z,
    lag_z = lag_z
  )
}

assign_quadrants <- function(sf_obj, z, lag_z) {
  sf_obj %>%
    mutate(
      z = z,
      lag_z = lag_z,
      quadrant = case_when(
        z > 0 & lag_z > 0 ~ "HH",
        z < 0 & lag_z < 0 ~ "LL",
        z > 0 & lag_z < 0 ~ "HL",
        z < 0 & lag_z > 0 ~ "LH",
        TRUE ~ "Axis"

    )
}

Step 6.5 Geographic Interpretations and Management Implications

The LISA maps reveal clear geographic patterns in dam clustering across the United States. High-high (HH) hotspots are concentrated in the eastern U.S., particularly in the Southeast and along major river systems such as the Tennessee River Basin. These regions contain dense networks of dams, potentially associated with large-scale water management systems like the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA). In contrast, low-low (LL) coldspots dominate much of the western U.S., especially in arid and mountainous regions where dam density is lower due to limited surface water availability and lower population density. These patterns reflect both natural hydrology and human water demand, with wetter regions supporting more infrastructure and drier regions relying on fewer, larger systems.

A clear example of an HH hotspot is the Tennessee Valley region. Dam clustering here can be explained by three key factors: a dense and interconnected river network that supports multiple dam sites, historical investment through the TVA which led to coordinated construction of dams for flood control, hydropower, and navigation, and high regional demand for electricity and water management, which required integrated infrastructure. These factors combine to produce a highly concentrated and spatially connected dam network, which is visible in both tessellations.

Some HL and LH tiles appear along the boundaries between high-density and low-density regions. These outliers may represent transitional zones where local conditions differ from surrounding areas. For example, a high-low (HL) tile may indicate a large dam or cluster of dams located in an otherwise low-density region, such as a major reservoir in the western U.S. Conversely, a low-high (LH) tile may occur in a sparsely developed area adjacent to a dense river system. These anomalies are important because they highlight locations where local infrastructure does not match regional trends, potentially requiring more insight.

The comparison between hexagons and counties demonstrates how MAUP affects local spatial analysis. While both units identify similar large-scale clustering patterns, counties produce more fragmented and irregular LISA classifications due to their uneven size and shape. This leads to more HL and LH classifications, suggesting greater variability. In contrast, hexagons provide a more stable representation of clustering. From a management perspective, HH hotspots indicate regions where coordinated water management efforts may be most important, while LL coldspots represent areas with simpler infrastructure and fewer management challenges. These findings are consistent with the patterns found in Question 4, where dam purposes also clustered regionally (irrigation in the West, hydropower in mountainous areas). Overall, LISA analysis highlights both broad regional patterns and localized anomalies, providing valuable insight for targeted policy and resource management.
